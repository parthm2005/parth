About Qutub Minar

Qutub Minar, located in Delhi, India, is the tallest brick minaret in the world. It was commissioned by Qutb-ud-din Aibak in 1193 and completed by his successor Iltutmish.

The minaret is 73 meters tall, with a diameter tapering from 14.3 meters at the base to 2.7 meters at the top. It is made of red sandstone and marble, with intricate carvings and verses from the Quran.

Qutub Minar is part of the Qutub Complex, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and a significant historical monument in India.

Architecture

The architecture of Qutub Minar is a fine example of Indo-Islamic Afghan architecture. The minaret is adorned with bands of inscriptions and is flanked by the famous Iron Pillar of Delhi, which has remained rust-free for over 1,600 years.

History

Qutub Minar was built to celebrate the victory of Muhammad Ghori over Rajput king Prithviraj Chauhan. It marked the beginning of Muslim rule in India. The construction of the minaret was started by Qutb-ud-din Aibak, the first ruler of the Delhi Sultanate, and was completed by his successor Iltutmish.
